Robert K. Greenleaf:

  • The heart of the non-Traditional Servant Leadership. Greenleaf wrote "The Servant as Leader," challenging conventional notions of leadership and paving the way for a more compassionate and people-centric approach. He is remembered with a clever epitaph (tombstone engraving): "Potentially a good plumber; ruined by a sophisticated education."
  • Greenleaf wrote "The Servant as Leader," challenging conventional notions of leadership and paving the way for a more compassionate and people-centric approach.
  • Born in 1904 in Terre Haute, influenced by his father, the original model for this transformative leadership style. The servant-leader shares power and places the needs of others first, extending not only to individuals but also to organizations as a whole, with the belief that both entities can embody the principles of servant leadership.
  • Greenleaf states that a good society is built on caring for one another, where the have and have not serve each other harmoniously.

James MacGregor Burns: A Pioneer in Transformational Leadership:

  • Born in Boston, Massachusetts. Burns was a Pulitzer Prize-winning presidential biographer obtaining a master's degree and a PhD in Government from Harvard.
  • Burns introduced the Theory of Transactional and Transformational Leadership leading to The University of Maryland eventually renaming its Academy of Leadership in his honor.
  • The essence of his theory centers on leaders and their followers elevating each other, in a way lesser context than the servant leadership style, fostering significant change in individuals and organizations.
  • Transformational leadership revolves around the leader's personality, traits, and ability to effect change through examples, articulation of an energizing vision, and setting challenging yet fulfilling goals.

Types of Theories

Trait Theories:

Trait Theories are categorized into three distinct types: cardinal, central, and secondary traits.

  • Cardinal traits: Rare, exert a strong influence on an individual's behavior, exemplified by well-known figures such as 'greedy Sam'
  • Central traits: Present in all individuals varying in ration. Significantly shapes behavior, yet do not entirely determine it. Examples are shyness, intelligence, deceitfulness, etc.
  • Secondary traits: Can impact behavior and heavily depend upon the present situation/context. Imagine a typically confident person becoming nervous when having to walk over unsteady foundation

Behavioral Theories of Leadership:

Focuses on observable behaviors and enables a objective and measurable analysis of leadership effectiveness, it considers situational context as well.

Task-Oriented Leadership:

Leaders concentrate on achieving goals and objectives. They provide clear instructions, set deadlines, and closely supervise their team members to ensure task completion.

People-Oriented Leadership:

Leaders prioritize the well-being and satisfaction of their team members. They demonstrate supportive and approachable behaviors, emphasizing the development of strong interpersonal relationships within the team.

Contingency Theories of Leadership:

These theories emphasize the absence of a one-size-fits-all approach to effective leadership, they are "contingent" on various situational factors:

  1. Fiedler's Contingency Model: Identifies specific situational factors that influence the effectiveness of a leader's actions, such as strong relationships between the leader and followers, well-defined tasks, and the level of formal authority granted to the leader.
  2. Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership Theory: Focuses on the readiness level of followers and how it impacts leadership behavior. Leaders need to adapt their leadership style based on the maturity and capability of their followers, ranging from directing and coaching to supporting and delegating.
  3. Vroom-Yetton Decision Model: Emphasizes the importance of decision-making styles. Depending on the situation's characteristics, leaders may choose autocratic, consultative, or participative decision-making styles to achieve optimal outcomes.

Understanding the interplay between leadership behaviors and situational factors enhances our ability to develop effective leaders capable of adapting to a diverse range of challenges and contexts.

Transformational Leadership:

Transformational leaders possess a dynamic and influential style that empowering their followers to achieve greatness. They create clear and compelling visions of the future, fostering creativity and innovation among their team members. These leaders prioritize the needs and development of each individual, employs effective communication and uses storytelling to ignite passion uniting the team.

They are committed to their own continuous development while actively nurturing the growth of their followers, exemplify integrity, and abide by the "down with the ship." principle

Servant Leadership:

Servant leadership centers on the act of serving others. It prioritizes the needs of followers, empowering them with resources, opportunities, and guidance to flourish. Servant leaders focus on the collective success of the team, fostering an environment of collaboration and shared achievement. Guided by high ethical standards they uphold an exceptional example for others to follow.

Attentive listening and genuine empathy shape this selfless leadership style building trust and fostering a cohesive organizational culture.
